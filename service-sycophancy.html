What It Is

The hidden bias that silently corrupts your RLHF pipeline

Sycophancy is when an AI model rewards or produces agreeable-but-wrong responses because the training signal of your RLHF annotations has systematically preferred them. It is one of the most dangerous and hardest-to-detect failure modes in LLM alignment.

Imagine a user asks: "I think the capital of Australia is Sydney am I right?" A sycophantic model says "Yes, absolutely! Sydney is a wonderful city." An aligned model says "Actually, the capital of Australia is Canberra, not Sydney a common misconception." The sycophantic answer is more agreeable. It feels nicer. And if your RLHF annotators are not specifically trained and tested to resist this preference, they will systematically reward the wrong answer.

The problem compounds in high-stakes domains. A medical AI that agrees with a patient's incorrect self-diagnosis because the patient stated it confidently is actively dangerous. A legal AI that validates a client's mistaken interpretation of a statute rather than correcting it causes real harm. A financial AI that confirms a user's bad investment thesis because they seem confident is a liability.

Sycophancy in RLHF annotation is not a rare edge case. Stanford and Anthropic research has shown that sycophancy is a systematic failure mode that emerges whenever annotators are not explicitly trained and tested to distinguish "agrees with me" from "is correct." Our audit measures your pipeline's actual susceptibility rate not with a theoretical estimate, but with real trap tasks run through your real annotators.

How does sycophancy get into RLHF data?
Annotators prefer responses that: (1) agree with the user's premise even when it's wrong, (2) validate the user's expressed opinion, (3) are written in a confident, authoritative tone even if less accurate, (4) sound more sophisticated even if less correct, (5) avoid contradiction even when contradiction is the right answer. Without specific training to resist these preferences, annotators systematically introduce sycophancy biases into preference data and those biases are then amplified by the reward model.
Why can't you just check your model outputs?
By the time sycophancy is visible in model outputs, it has already been baked into your reward model through thousands of corrupted preference pairs. Checking model outputs tells you you have a problem but not how deep the contamination in your training data is, which annotation tasks are most affected, or which annotators are most susceptible. Our audit answers all three questions before you scale your pipeline.
Is this a one-time audit or ongoing?
The initial audit is a fixed-price, one-time engagement that establishes your baseline susceptibility score and delivers corrective data. After completing a corrective training batch (which we can also provide), a follow-up audit 4–6 weeks later measures whether annotator calibration has improved. Ongoing sycophancy monitoring is available as part of our Continuous Model Quality Retainer service.

Trap Categories

Eight types of sycophancy traps we inject and measure

Each trap type tests a different dimension of annotator susceptibility. Your overall susceptibility score is a weighted composite across all eight categories.

1. False Premise Validation
Prompt: "Since Australia's capital is Sydney, what other famous landmarks are there?" Response A correctly says "Canberra is the capital" Response B agrees with Sydney and lists landmarks.
Does your annotator reward the response that corrects the false premise, or the one that goes along with it? The most common form of sycophancy and the most dangerous in factual domains.
2. Opinion Capitulation
User states a strong opinion. Response A maintains a different well-reasoned position. Response B agrees with the user's opinion and abandons its prior reasoning.
Tests whether annotators reward intellectual independence or social conformity. Models trained on capitulation-rewarding data learn to change their position whenever a user pushes back, regardless of who is right.
3. Authority Inflation
Prompt contains "As a professor of medicine, I believe X..." where X is medically incorrect. Response A respectfully disagrees. Response B defers to the claimed authority and validates X.
Tests whether annotators are more likely to reward deference to claimed expertise even when the claimed expert is wrong. Critical for medical, legal, and scientific AI applications.
4. Flattery Reward
Response A: accurate, direct, somewhat critical of user's approach. Response B: heavily flatters the user's approach while giving the same or worse actual advice.
Tests whether annotators reward flattery as a proxy for helpfulness. Models trained this way learn to open with compliments and validate user decisions even when honest feedback would be more helpful.
5. Confidence Mimicry
Response A: accurate content, appropriately hedged ("studies suggest..."). Response B: less accurate content but written with total confidence and no hedging.
Tests whether annotators mistake confident tone for accurate content. Models trained this way learn to remove uncertainty signals to sound more authoritative, the opposite of good epistemic calibration.
6. Contradiction Avoidance
Earlier in a conversation the user stated X. Now X is relevant and incorrect. Response A contradicts the user's earlier statement. Response B avoids contradiction and works around it.
Tests whether annotators reward consistency-with-user over truth. Multi-turn conversation testing. Critical for dialogue systems that need to maintain factual integrity across long conversations.
7. Excessive Qualification
Response A gives a clear direct answer. Response B hedges so heavily to avoid any possible disagreement that it becomes vague and unhelpful "It depends... there are many perspectives... ultimately you should decide..."
Tests whether annotators reward over-hedging as a form of political safety. Models trained this way become useless in domains where clear, direct answers are needed.
8. Emotional Flattery
User expresses strong emotional attachment to a decision. Response A gently challenges it with evidence. Response B validates the emotional investment and avoids challenging the decision.
Tests whether annotators prefer emotional validation over honest assessment. Particularly dangerous for AI tools in financial planning, medical decision-making, and legal advice contexts.

Common Questions

What teams ask before the audit

Do I need to give you access to my model or training infrastructure?
No. The audit operates entirely at the annotation pipeline and data level. We need: access to your annotation platform (or the ability to submit tasks through it), your current annotation guidelines, and a sample of 50–100 recent preference pairs for calibration. We do not need your model weights, training infrastructure, or proprietary architecture details.
Will annotators know they are being tested?
No and this is deliberate. Annotators who know they are being tested for sycophancy will suppress their natural tendencies and produce artificially clean results. Our traps are indistinguishable from normal annotation tasks. This gives you the real susceptibility rate, not the rate when annotators are performing for an audit. All annotators are informed (after the fact) that trap tasks are a standard part of our quality monitoring this is disclosed in annotator agreements.
What does a "good" sycophancy rate look like?
For general-purpose domains, a susceptibility rate below 15% is considered acceptable. For high-stakes domains (medical, legal, financial), the acceptable threshold is below 10%. Most pipelines we audit for the first time score between 25–45% which means roughly one in three to one in four preference judgments is sycophantically biased. After calibration and corrective training pairs, typical improvement is 15–25 percentage points in the follow-up audit.
